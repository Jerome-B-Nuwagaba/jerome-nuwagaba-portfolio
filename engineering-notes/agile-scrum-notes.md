# Agile and Scrum

## Agile;

Agile is a software development philosophy that prioritizes flexibility, collaboration,
and delivering working software in small, frequent increments rather than one large
release at the end. It was formalized in 2001 through the **Agile Manifesto**, which
outlines four core values and twelve principles.


## Agile Principles (Key Ones)

- Deliver working software frequently (weeks rather than months)
- Welcome changing requirements, even late in development
- Business stakeholders and developers must work together daily
- Build projects around motivated individuals and trust them
- Working software is the primary measure of progress
- Maintain a sustainable, constant pace of development
- Continuous attention to technical excellence improves agility
- Simplicity — maximizing the work not done — is essential

## Scrum?

Scrum is the most widely used **Agile framework**. It organizes work into fixed-length
cycles called **Sprints** (usually 1–4 weeks), with defined roles, events, and
artifacts that keep the team aligned and moving forward.

## Scrum Roles

### Product Owner
- Represents the stakeholders and the business
- Owns and prioritizes the **Product Backlog**
- Decides what gets built and in what order
- Responsible for maximizing the value of the product

### Scrum Master
- Facilitates the Scrum process
- Removes blockers that slow the team down
- Coaches the team on Agile principles
- Does NOT manage the team — acts as a servant leader

### Development Team
- Cross-functional group that does the actual work
- Self-organizing — they decide how to achieve the Sprint Goal
- Typically 3–9 people

## Scrum Events

### Sprint
- A fixed time period (1–4 weeks) in which a usable product increment is built
- Once started, the Sprint Goal does not change
- At the end, working software must be ready to ship

### Sprint Planning
- Happens at the start of each Sprint
- Team selects items from the Product Backlog and commits to a Sprint Goal
- Team breaks selected items into tasks

### Daily Scrum (Standup)
- 15-minute daily meeting
- Each team member answers three questions:
  1. What did I do yesterday?
  2. What will I do today?
  3. Are there any blockers?

### Sprint Review
- Held at the end of the Sprint
- Team demonstrates completed work to stakeholders
- Feedback is gathered and the backlog is updated

### Sprint Retrospective
- Also at the end of the Sprint, after the Review
- Team reflects on their process, not the product
- Answers: What went well? What didn't? What do we improve?

## Scrum Artifacts

### Product Backlog
- A prioritized list of all features, fixes, and improvements needed
- Owned by the Product Owner
- Always evolving — never truly complete

### Sprint Backlog
- The subset of the Product Backlog selected for the current Sprint
- Owned by the Development Team

### Increment
- The sum of all completed Product Backlog items at the end of a Sprint
- Must meet the team's **Definition of Done**

## How Agile Relates to SDLC

Traditional SDLC runs phases once in sequence. Agile runs through a mini-SDLC
in every Sprint:

- **Planning** → Sprint Planning
- **Design + Implementation** → Sprint execution
- **Testing** → Continuous, within the Sprint
- **Deployment** → Potentially shippable increment every Sprint
- **Review** → Sprint Review and Retrospective

This makes Agile faster to adapt, easier to course-correct, and more aligned
with real user needs.

## Key Takeaway

Agile is a mindset; Scrum is a framework that puts that mindset into practice.
As an engineer at Otic, understanding Scrum means you can contribute effectively
to sprint planning, daily standups, and retrospectives — and understand why the
team works the way it does.
